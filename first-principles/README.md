# First principles & the polymath mind

Most of what you "know" you actually borrowed — a convention, a best practice, a way
things have always been done. That's efficient, until the borrowed answer is wrong for
your situation and you can't tell, because you never traced it back to anything solid.
This module is about the two habits that fix that: **reasoning from fundamentals** instead
of by analogy, and **building enough range** that you have more than one discipline's
fundamentals to reason from.

The first half is a method. First-principles thinking has a bad reputation as a genius
party trick; it's actually a boring, repeatable procedure — deconstruct a problem to
things that must be true, challenge every assumption you find, and rebuild. The second
half is a posture: the **polymath** isn't someone with a freakish IQ, but someone who has
deliberately collected the foundational models of several fields and learned to move ideas
between them.

- [**What first-principles thinking actually is**](./what-is-first-principles.md) —
  reasoning from fundamentals vs. reasoning by analogy, and why the difference decides
  whether you can ever beat the consensus.
- [**The method: deconstruct, challenge, reconstruct**](./the-method.md) — a repeatable
  procedure with concrete tools: Socratic questioning, the 5 Whys, and Fermi estimation.
- [**A latticework of mental models**](./mental-models-latticework.md) — why a handful of
  big ideas from many disciplines beats one field's toolkit, and a starter set to carry.
- [**Becoming a polymath**](./becoming-a-polymath.md) — range vs. depth, T- and
  comb-shaped expertise, and how transfer between fields actually happens.
- [**Learning how to learn**](./learning-how-to-learn.md) — deliberate practice, the
  Feynman technique, retrieval practice, and spacing — the engine that makes breadth
  affordable.
- [**Traps & limits**](./traps-and-limits.md) — biases that masquerade as reasoning, when
  analogy beats first principles, and how to avoid reinventing solved problems.

The two halves reinforce each other: first-principles thinking is the *method*, and a
broad latticework of models is the *raw material* the method works on. A method with no
material reasons in a vacuum; material with no method just collects trivia.

**📌 Close out the module:** [Recap & real-world examples](./recap.md) — how first-principles
reasoning showed up in real breakthroughs and real failures, plus the key takeaways.
